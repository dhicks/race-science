This section uses simulated data to provide a proof-of-concept for our use of topic models to detect the “mainstreaming” of a marginalized topic.

k = 20   ## total number of topics
N = 100  ## papers per journal

We construct a simulated corpus as follows. We work with five journals, labelled A through E. Journal A primarily publishes the marginalized topic (correspond to race science). Journals C-E are mainstream journals, and publish an even mix from 19 other topics. Journal B is the venue by which the marginalized topic is “mainstreamed.” This journal is represented in a time-indexed way, \(B_t\), with \(t\) ranging from 0 to 5.

This gives us three sets of journals: A, B (0-5), and C-E. In the LDA model, these journal sets correspond to \(\mathbf{\alpha}\), the parameter for the “prior” Dirichlet distribution \(\Theta(\mathbf{\alpha})\). The tmfast package provides a helper function peak_alpha for constructing \(\mathbf{\alpha}\).

500 words each

doc_len = rep.int(500, N * length(alphas))

Draw corpus —-

tic() corpus = draw_corpus(doc_len, theta, phi) |> mutate(doc = rownames(theta)[doc], n = log1p(n)) toc()

Fit topic model —-

fitted = tmfast(corpus, c(10, 15, 20, 25, 30))

Topics by journal —-

tidy(fitted, k = 20, matrix = ‘gamma’) |> mutate(journal = str_extract(document, ’1*[0-9]?‘)) |> ggplot(aes(document, topic, fill = gamma)) + geom_tile() + facet_wrap(vars(journal), scales = ’free_x’)

Topic model clearly detects the focal topic in A, increase in B over time, minimal presence in other journals
